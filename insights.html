Insights

Notes from the edge of electric aviation operations.

Short perspectives on why physics-first intelligence matters when route history and failure statistics do not yet exist — for operators, underwriters, and the wider advanced air mobility ecosystem.

Thesis

Operational intelligence

Physics before fleet history

Conventional aviation analytics assumes decades of accumulated operating evidence. Electric aircraft enter service without that corpus. Route planning, insurance, financing, and dispatch still need decision-grade numbers on day one.

AeroMind starts from multi-domain models of the aircraft and environment — electrochemistry, thermal behaviour, aerodynamics — and treats telemetry as continuous calibration, not a prerequisite. That inversion is what makes corridor economics and per-aircraft state-of-health usable before actuarial precedent exists.

Insurers & lessors

Risk & capital

Underwriting without actuarial precedent

Hull and liability pricing for novel electric platforms cannot lean solely on historical loss tables. What underwriters and lessors need is calibrated condition evidence per airframe — with quantified uncertainty — from first flight through the lease life.

Independent operational intelligence closes the gap between OEM performance claims and the capital providers who must live with residual risk for a decade.

Operators & cities

Network planning

Corridors that do not exist yet

Network design for advanced air mobility is not a simple transfer of airline city-pair heuristics. Physics-feasible range, energy, utilisation, and infrastructure constraints define which corridors can actually pay.

Evaluating candidate networks before revenue service — under transparent aircraft and energy assumptions — is how operators and city planners avoid building for demand that the vehicle cannot serve.
